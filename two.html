Anxiety disorders in teenagers include generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), social anxiety disorder, panic disorder, and specific phobias. These disorders are characterized by chronic, excessive worry or fear that interferes with daily activities. These feelings of extreme fear and nervousness sometimes impedes activities that you once never had a second thought about.

Common Signs: Excessive fear and worry may be factors of these disorders. But there are also other common symptoms found in patients suffering these disorders.

  1. GAD and Social Anxiety Disorder: Along with excessive worriness about various activities, GAD may also cause you to feel extreme fatigue, unrestlensess, sleep disturbances and difficulty concentrating. Symptoms of social anxiety disorder include situations where you feel nervous of being judged around certain groups of people, to the point where these feelings start affecting your emotions, and bodily signs such as sweating and dizziness.
  2. Panic Disorder: Frequent moments where intense fear suddenly fills up your entire feelings. These feelings lead to physical symptoms like tight chest, difficulty breathing, and heart palpitations

Causes: Different events and/or experiences lead to the feelings of worry and fear. Sometimes, recurring events start to build up these emotions to the point where you may actually be suffering from one of the previously mentioned disorders.

  1. Stressful Life Events: These events may sometimes look like nothing to the common bystander, but may have significant value to different people. This includes academic pressure and family issues.
  2. Genetic Factors And brain chemistry: These factors are common when analyzing causes of different mental health illnesses. Family history of anxiety disorders sometimes increase your chance of suffering from the same disorders, and different brain chemicals may affect your overall brain chemistry such as imbalances in neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine.

Combat (Treatment): Different treatments can be used to combat anxiety disorders, and the effectiveness of each treatment is going to differ for each person, so don't be discouraged if one doesn't work out for you.

  1. Psychotherapy and Medication: Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) is effective in helping teens identify and change negative thought patterns and behaviors. Exposure therapy, a component of CBT, gradually exposes individuals to nxiety-provoking situations to reduce fear. In addition, certain medications may also be of use to reduce feelings of extreme fear. To illustrate, SSRIs (ex: Zoloft, Prozac) and benzodiazepines are commonly prescribed to manage anxiety symptoms
  2. Lab tests: Although not as often used, these lab tests can be used to find out other medical disorders that may be leading to the expansion of anxiety disorders.
  3. Support Groups: As always, expressing your feelings usually helps people let loose, and avoid holding on to many negative thoughts which in the long run could psychologically affect you greatly. To exemplify, engaging with trusted friends, family, or mental health professionals provides emotional support and practical advice.
